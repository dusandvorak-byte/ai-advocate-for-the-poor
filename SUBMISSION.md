# AI Advocate for the Poor — OpenAI Build Week submission draft

**Creator and project lead: Mgr. Dušan Dvořák**

## One-line pitch

AI Advocate for the Poor turns an overwhelming archive into an auditable memory of a case — giving people without money, health, or institutional power the structure they need to be heard.

## The unfair advantage we are removing

Justice often depends on something that is never named in a statute: **who can hold the whole case in memory**. An institution has a file, staff, case numbers, deadlines, and continuity. A person in poverty, living with disability, in prison, or supporting a family member may have the evidence but not the capacity to connect it.

This is the project's deeper idea: AI should not merely answer the last question asked. It should preserve the provenance of thousands of elements, organize them into meaningful branches, and let a human move from the entire archive to the one evidential path that matters now.

## The story since 1994: one idea, a new generation of tools

The journey began in Olomouc in 1994 with the development of P-centrum, a center for the prevention, treatment, and rehabilitation of people affected by drug dependency. It was more than a professional service: the project connected care with a gallery, supported work, programs for children, and a route back into ordinary life. The principle behind today's product was already present — a person is not a problem to be processed, but a human being with a story, capabilities, and dignity.

The next step moved access to help online. InternetPoradna connected people seeking guidance with a large volunteer community of professionals and could lead to regional, in-person support. It demonstrated that technology can overcome distance, disability, and lack of money when a real human network remains behind it.

The continuity is also recorded in a written statement issued on September 14, 2010 by Martin Novotný, then Mayor of the City of Olomouc. He expressly attributed to Dušan Dvořák the founding of P-centrum and the U mloka gallery and authorship of InternetPoradna, which his statement described as employing people with disabilities, educating university students, and providing professional advice to the public. This is presented as a direct official statement by the mayor, not as a judicial finding or an independent audit of each outcome.

From 2005 to 2009, that experience developed into Ateliér ALF, an open, barrier-free adult school in Prague. New technologies, visual communication, and social and legal guidance were not ends in themselves. They were tools for education, employment, and inclusion of people facing serious health or social disadvantage. The alliance's annual report records more than 500 people directly supported; a contemporary Czech Ministry of Labour and Social Affairs document presented Ateliér ALF as a good-practice example and identified Dušan Dvořák as its director.

From 2008, the Educational Cannabis Clinic and the Cannabis is the Cure research program grew from this infrastructure. Alongside education and counselling came a large archive of scientific, administrative, and judicial documents. That experience exposed another form of inequality: a person can preserve important records for decades and still lose the case's meaning unless the archive has structure, relationships, provenance, and continuity.

In December 2009, the Czech Government Board for People with Disabilities awarded Dušan Dvořák second prize for the Cannabis is the Cure website in its category for online journalistic work addressing disability. The award documents a long-standing connection between internet communication, public education, and the needs of people with disabilities.

A preserved letter from the Committee of Good Will – Olga Havel Foundation, dated April 21, 2011 (ref. 218/2011/gt), records its board's decision to provide CZK 25,000 to the Educational Cannabis Clinic for a named research project. It is evidence of institutional support for the project; it is not presented as evidence that a treatment was effective or that any medical conclusion was correct.

Another layer of the story is independently documented by the Czech Ministry of Defence's [official register of certificates](https://3odboj.mo.gov.cz/ucastnici-odboje-odporu-proti-komunismu/vydana-osvedceni), which lists Mgr. Dušan Dvořák as a participant in resistance and opposition to communism. A [contemporaneous report dated February 9, 2021](https://www.impuls.cz/regiony/olomoucky-kraj/dusan-dvorak-odboj-proti-komunismu-vezeni-konopi-na-leky.A210209_045203_imp-olomoucky_kov/tisk), quoting the responsible Ministry of Defence department director, records that the certificate reached him while he was serving a prison sentence in Břeclav and identifies the recognized activity as occurring from 1987 to November 17, 1989. This is biographical context, not evidence that any later legal claim in the cannabis case is correct.

The family memory also includes his grandfather František Dvořák's resistance to both Nazi and communist rule. This is a separate historical line: the project will not conflate it with Dušan Dvořák's certificate or publish claims from private family records without separate archival and official verification.

AI Advocate for the Poor is the 2026 continuation of that work — not an isolated technology experiment:

**safe place → accessible advice → barrier-free education → living archive → auditable memory with AI**

What once required a building, hundreds of volunteers, and years of manual work can become accessible from a phone: upload documents or speak in one's own language, preserve the source of every claim, control what may be shared, and hand a professional a navigable case. The technology changes. The mission remains: freedom, human dignity, and practical help without distinction.

## The problem

A person may have a legitimate claim and important evidence yet still fail because they lack the money, health, time, or professional capacity to organize hundreds of documents, events, people, institutions, case references, and deadlines. Institutions have structured files and professional staff. A disadvantaged person often arrives with an unmanageable pile of material.

## The solution: from document pile to living evidence map

The project is building a long-term, source-grounded memory for a legal case. Its first public stage:

- maps multiple text documents locally without uploading them;
- identifies documents, case references, dates, and factual or procedural branches;
- provides a one-click fictional demo case with no personal data;
- presents a public, consent-based living-memory case study alongside a separate anonymized supported sample;
- maps 70 recorded decisions or procedural acts from 2010–2024 (31 first-instance, 10 Supreme Court, and 29 Constitutional Court nodes) while explicitly warning that this is not a claim of 70 separate cases;
- keeps three active intervention-action branches before the Prague Municipal Court and the civil Czech Television lawsuit separate, each with a source and verification status;
- provides a one-click anonymized institutional map of a 2004–2026 European case, connecting the President, courts, public prosecutors, ministries, police, forensic bodies, European institutions, compensation, and the research program;
- shows a timeline and headline counts while distinguishing indexed material from creator-stated figures still requiring an anonymized primary source;
- separates extracted facts, legal interpretation, uncertainty, and recommended next steps for one anonymized supported sample;
- displays an exact source quotation with every conclusion;
- refuses to transfer the prepared interpretation to altered, unrelated, or multi-document inputs.

The target workflow is:

**documents → people, institutions, events and proceedings → evidence and contradictions → the relevant branch → a cited next step → a draft for human review**

That architecture has value far beyond one legal dispute. It can help a family reconstruct a prisoner's case, a disabled person maintain continuity through years of administrative proceedings, a legal-aid clinic triage a large archive, or an investigative team preserve the chain from source to conclusion. The scarce resource is not text generation. It is trustworthy structure.

## The zero-barrier intake vision

The person should not need legal vocabulary — or even be able to type. They can choose the safest path available to them:

1. upload documents for local preprocessing and guided redaction; or
2. tell their story by voice, in their own language, one small step at a time.

The system converts either path into the same reviewable structure: chronology, people and institutions, proceedings, claims, supporting passages, missing evidence, uncertainty, and urgent deadlines. The person confirms what may be shared. Only then can an authorized lawyer or legal-aid worker review the anonymized case, identify the appropriate route, and return a proposed next step.

This is especially important for people affected by disability, literacy barriers, trauma, imprisonment, language barriers, or digital exclusion. Voice and multilingual access are not convenience features; they are part of equal access. They are a next-stage vision and are not claimed as working features of the current static prototype.

## Why this can change access to justice

This is not another chatbot designed to answer a legal question confidently. A chatbot forgets the archive and optimizes for an answer. AI Advocate is designed to preserve the archive and optimize for an **auditable path from evidence to action**.

- **Memory as infrastructure:** a case survives exhaustion, illness, imprisonment, staff changes, and the passage of years.
- **Evidence before eloquence:** every displayed claim carries the passage that supports it.
- **Procedural truth:** a referral, acknowledgment, allegation, finding, and final decision remain different things.
- **Human control:** uncertainty is visible and proposed action remains reviewable.
- **Transferability:** a lawyer or nonprofit receives a navigable case, not an unexplained AI answer.
- **Dignity:** the person is no longer reduced to a disorganized stack of documents.

The public case study is deliberately demanding: one person's documentary memory across institutions and years. It shows why careful archives matter and why a model without ordered, source-grounded context cannot produce equally careful work.

## How Codex and GPT-5.6 were used

GPT-5.6 helped translate a broad social and legal problem into a clear product concept, examine likely confusions between facts, allegations, procedural actions, and legal conclusions, and design a source-grounded output structure. Codex audited the repository and implemented the input boundary, local case mapping, interface, adversarial regression tests, documentation, and restricted GitHub Pages deployment.

No model runs inside the current static demo. That is a deliberate first-stage boundary: prepared conclusions must not leak onto unsupported documents. A later stage will place GPT-5.6 behind input validation, quotation checks, a strict output schema, and human review.

This project existed as an early prototype before the Submission Period. The work created during OpenAI Build Week is documented by the dated commit history and the primary Codex build thread. During the Submission Period, Codex and GPT-5.6 were used to turn it into a materially safer and more complete product: strict document identity checks, multi-document case mapping, a bilingual interface, a source-grounded living-memory registry, adversarial tests, repository cleanup, and a restricted web-only deployment.

## Safety is part of the product

The prototype clearly states that it is neither legal advice nor a universal legal AI. It has two separately bounded prepared-input paths: the verified KSZ referral sample and one exact court PDF concerning an association-governance change. The PDF is checked locally by SHA-256; the private original is never uploaded or published, and only an anonymized derivative is public. A procedural referral is never presented as confirmation of wrongdoing. Unknown PDFs receive no prepared interpretation, and multi-document text mode produces an orientation map only.

## Current limitations

The public version accepts text for technical mapping and exactly one versioned PDF plus its anonymized derivative for a second prepared test. It does not yet include general PDF/DOCX analysis, OCR, a database, authentication, universal legal analysis, or automated filing. These are planned capabilities, not claims about the current build.

## Demo video script — 2 minutes 30 seconds maximum

1. **0–25 seconds — the invisible inequality:** “Institutions have structured files and professional memory. A disadvantaged person may have the same evidence in hundreds of documents — and still be unable to make it usable.”
2. **25–55 seconds — instant structure:** Load the fictional case and create its map. Show documents becoming branches, references, dates, and relationships.
3. **55–90 seconds — living memory:** Show the public case-study registry. Explain the distinction between an official statement, the author's allegation, a legal principle, and an unresolved contradiction. Open a source quotation.
4. **90–125 seconds — safety as a feature:** Load the KSZ Ostrava sample and state: “A referral is not proof of wrongdoing.” Show the draft before the test and the sent output after it. Then load the public anonymized PDF copy, compare memory before and after insertion, show the generated solution and relevance traffic light, and explain that the private original is matched locally by fingerprint while an unknown PDF is rejected.
5. **125–140 seconds — Codex and GPT-5.6:** With narration, show the dated Build Week commits, tests, and README. Explain how Codex accelerated implementation and how GPT-5.6 supported product and safety decisions.
6. **140–150 seconds — close:** “We are not replacing a lawyer. We are giving every person the memory, structure, and dignity needed to reach one.”

The final video must be in English (or include a complete English translation), contain spoken audio, be publicly visible on YouTube, and remain under three minutes.
