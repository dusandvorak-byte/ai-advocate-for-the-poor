# AI Advocate for the Poor — OpenAI Build Week submission

**Creator and project lead: Mgr. Dušan Dvořák**

## When you know what happened but cannot yet prove it

An official letter arrives on Friday afternoon. It contains reference numbers, links to earlier proceedings and perhaps a deadline. You do not know when the clock started. The person who could advise you is unavailable until next week.

Or your house burns down. A pile of damaged documents, photographs and receipts remains. The police, the fire service and the insurer each have their own file, but nobody has the whole story.

Or your grandmother dies and your large family is scattered around the world. Everyone knows a different part of her life and the family’s history. Photographs, letters, official records and memories remain, but nobody knows who holds what or how to connect the pieces into a shared family archive.

Or there is an accident. You know what you saw, but you face reports, expert opinions, photographs, witness statements and claims that change over time.

At that moment, you are not a lawyer, archivist or investigator. Yet you still need answers to four ordinary questions: **What happened? What does the evidence show? What remains unknown? What must a person check?**

This is where AI Advocate for the Poor begins: between **“I know what happened”** and **“I can prove it.”** It does not add another polished summary or a confident answer without memory. It identifies where a new document belongs, what it changes in the existing story, which other records it affects and where the system must say, “I do not know.”

This is not only about people who are poor in money. In the face of a complex system, anyone can become poor — in time, health, information, professional support or the ability to keep the whole story connected.

**AI Advocate turns an overwhelming archive into auditable memory, so a person does not have to be wealthy, healthy or legally trained to understand their own case.**

## Why I created it

I am a special educator, addiction specialist, and group and family therapist. I am not a lawyer or an IT specialist. Since the beginning of this century, I have worked on research into cannabis therapy, its practical application and accessibility, while connecting digital technology with professional support for people with disabilities. Since 2004, I have had to preserve the continuity of a large archive of criminal, administrative, constitutional and European proceedings concerning cannabis research, cultivation and medical use.

Proceedings aimed at restricting my legal capacity took place in 2012 and 2016; my capacity was not restricted. In 2019, I was sentenced to an aggregate term of 80 months in prison. I am also officially recognised as having a third-degree disability, the highest degree under the Czech system.

More than twenty lawyers represented me, and I spent approximately CZK 1.5 million on legal assistance. Yet every professional and every authority saw only one part. I still had to hold together the full history, its contradictions, its unanswered questions and the effect of every new document.

On 20 April 2026, we began processing this archive with artificial intelligence. For the first time, the result was not merely another summary. AI helped make a clean, surgical cut through the evidence and the history of a twenty-year judicial slalom — including five legislative proposals — and turn it into a sourced, reviewable structure. My case became the project’s living test bench, not the limit of its usefulness.

## Thirty years in the same direction

The project did not appear overnight as a competition idea. It was more than a professional service. The emerging space included the U mloka gallery, supported employment, programmes for children and a route back into ordinary life. Even then, the underlying principle was the same as it is today: a person is not a problem to be recorded, but the bearer of a story, abilities and dignity.

The next step brought assistance online. InternetPoradna connected people seeking advice with a large volunteer community of professionals and could also lead them to regional, in-person support. It showed that technology can overcome distance, disability and lack of money — if a real network of people remains behind it.

He expressly attributes to Dušan Dvořák the founding of P-centrum and the U mloka gallery, the creation of InternetPoradna — which, according to his statement, employed people with disabilities, educated university students and provided professional advice to the public — and the organisation of public-awareness activities. This is a direct official statement by the Mayor, not a court judgment or an independent audit of each individual outcome.

New technologies, visual communication, and social and legal counselling were not ends in themselves. In 2026, AI Advocate for the Poor is the next step. It is not an isolated technology experiment. It is the digital continuation of thirty years of work:

**safe place → accessible advice → barrier-free education → living archive → auditable memory with AI**

What once required a building, hundreds of volunteers and years of manual work can now be made available to a person through a phone: upload documents or speak in their own language, preserve the origin of every claim, decide safely what may be shared and hand a comprehensible case to a professional. Technology changes. The purpose remains the same: freedom, human dignity and practical help without discrimination.

## Added value beyond existing systems

Most legal AI tools begin once a lawyer already has a defined case. They assist with legal research, analysis and drafting. Case-management systems organise client matters that have already been opened. Electronic discovery tools review documents within a dispute whose boundaries are already known.

AI Advocate begins earlier: with the person who does not yet have a legal team, a complete file or an institution preserving their whole story. Its basic unit is not a chatbot answer, but a traceable relationship between a claim and its source.

The system is designed to show over time:

- who said what and when;
- which document supports the claim;
- whether the record contains an allegation, referral, investigation, finding or final decision;
- where a claim or position changed;
- which document is missing;
- what has not yet been established;
- how the arrival of a new record changes related timelines, submissions and procedural branches.

The practical difference from conventional tools is:

- **Memory instead of a one-off answer:** a new document enters a long-term timeline, relationship map and set of procedural branches.
- **Impact instead of an isolated summary:** the system shows the state before and after insertion, which submissions the change affects and where it must not be propagated.
- **Evidence discipline instead of a persuasive tone:** facts, claims, interpretation and uncertainty remain separate; conclusions are linked to sources and unsupported input is rejected.
- **Privacy by default:** preprocessing and verification can take place locally, publication is controlled and third-party data is anonymised.
- **Human hand-off instead of professional replacement:** the user, adviser, journalist or lawyer receives a navigable case and a list of what still requires verification.

This memory should belong to the person, not to a platform or institution.

## What works in the current prototype

The public bilingual version is a safely bounded demonstration prototype, not a universal legal AI. It currently:

- maps multiple text records locally without sending them to a server;
- identifies documents, dates, case references, and substantive or procedural branches;
- displays a public case study of living memory with the creator’s consent;
- provides a map of 70 recorded decisions or procedural acts from 2010–2024 — 31 first-instance acts, 10 Supreme Court decisions and 29 Constitutional Court decisions — without claiming that these are 70 separate cases;
- keeps three administrative actions alleging unlawful intervention before the Municipal Court in Prague separate from a civil action against Czech Television;
- provides an anonymised institutional map of the 2004–2026 European case;
- separates facts, claims, interpretation, uncertainty and recommended next steps;
- displays source citations for prepared conclusions;
- refuses to transfer a prepared legal interpretation to modified, foreign or unsupported input.

The intended workflow is:

**records → people, institutions, events and proceedings → claims, evidence, contradictions and gaps → relevant branch → cited next step → draft for human review**

## Tested on real documents

Since 20 April 2026, we have tested the working method behind the prototype on real documents and submissions. Immediately before the competition deadline, we conducted two controlled tests of the public version. Their purpose was not to produce an impressive answer, but to determine whether one new record could safely change an existing case memory and produce a reviewable proposed solution.

### 1. Internal test of an already populated archive

A notice from the Regional Public Prosecutor’s Office in Ostrava, dated 8 July 2026 and received on 16 July, was added to the connected case memory. The system correctly classified it as the procedural referral of part of a submission, not confirmation of wrongdoing. It created a new open branch for the District Public Prosecutor’s Office in Frýdek-Místek, separated the issue date from the receipt date, did not confuse a voluntary monitoring checkpoint with a statutory deadline and used a traffic-light system to distinguish direct, conditional and zero relevance to other proceedings.

The new record was compared with three drafts that had not yet been filed. The prototype proposed a different addition for each and detected two risky formulations that prematurely stated that certain materials had already been filed or sent.

After human review, the creator sent three final documents on 19 July 2026 and approved their publication exactly as sent. The system did not file any document itself and does not describe any output as delivered without proof of delivery.

### 2. Outside-input test

An exactly versioned court-register PDF was inserted locally into the public demonstration. The prototype verified its digital fingerprint, kept the contents of the court order separate from the archive owner’s statement, extracted only the relevant change in the association’s governing body and showed the memory before and after insertion.

It did not infer legal finality, general signing authority or any effect on unrelated legal branches. The private original was neither uploaded nor published; only an anonymised derivative extract is public. The system rejects an unknown PDF instead of transferring the prepared analysis to it.

### Result

All 54 automated tests passed. They cover the exact identity of supported inputs, rejection of altered and foreign documents, source citations, the distinction between referral and confirmation of wrongdoing, correct Czech public-prosecution terminology, parity between the Czech and English interfaces, anonymisation and the provenance of published outputs.

The result does not establish universal legal analysis. It demonstrates a limited and auditable process that can safely place a new record, show its systemic effect and mark what a person must verify.

## Where the system can be used

Most people do not want to change an entire system. They simply need to organise their own situation.

### Family, inheritance and genealogy

A family dispersed around the world can connect memories, photographs, letters, a family tree and property records in a private archive stored on its own devices. Following the death of a relative, the system can show what the family has, what is missing and what a person or professional must verify.

### Fire, flood, accident and insurance

The system can sort surviving documents, photographs, testimony, receipts, police records and insurer materials. It can build a timeline and identify missing evidence. A policyholder, insurer, police officer or professional investigator can work from the same structure, but each sees only the data they are authorised to access.

### Legal, social and psychological support

A person will be able to describe their story anonymously by voice. The system will ask questions step by step and prepare an orientation map, material for an advisory service and a list of details that a professional must verify.

Building on the experience of InternetPoradna, a network of online advice services can connect safe initial orientation with human advisory centres, psychological support and online consultations across countries and languages. Recommendations will not be a paid ranking or a claim about who will “definitely help best”. They will be based on publicly verifiable criteria such as specialisation, language, accessibility, video consultations, cost and availability. Actual suitability and capacity must be confirmed by the user and provider.

If a description indicates an immediate risk to life or health, the system must interrupt ordinary recommendations and prioritise verified options for immediate human help. It does not replace a crisis worker, psychologist, doctor or emergency service.

### Investigative work, media and nonprofit organisations

A journalist can receive a source-linked chronology of a systemic failure; a person still writes the article and remains responsible for it. A nonprofit organisation can connect permits, measurements, maps, decisions, budgets and public commitments, then show where actual conduct departed from documented commitments.

### War and religious conflict

A later specialised version could help preserve multilingual testimony, photographs, maps, orders and damaged archives. Such use requires collaboration with expert organisations, witness protection and a strict separation of testimony, allegation, propaganda and verified fact.

## What funding will make possible

Funding will not be used to build a larger chat window. It will create a shared factual core with four practical modes above it:

1. private personal and family memory;
2. an accessible advisory service with voice input;
3. a controlled evidence room for collaboration;
4. professional outputs for lawyers, journalists, nonprofit organisations, insurers, investigators and legislators.

The principal development tracks are:

- intake of PDFs, scans, photographs and audio testimony;
- recognition of metadata, dates, people, institutions and case references;
- duplicate detection and preservation of document provenance;
- a relationship graph of people, events, institutions, documents and claims;
- mandatory citations and separation of fact, allegation, opinion, hypothesis and decision;
- voice control, plain language and one-question-at-a-time interaction;
- checks for missing information and cautious treatment of possible deadlines;
- encrypted sharing, precise permissions and change history;
- exports tailored to the needs of a specific professional;
- security, factual, accessibility and user testing.

Deadlines must never be guessed. The system will label them as confirmed, probable, incomplete or potentially urgent, cite the basis for the calculation and flag missing information such as a delivery date.

Where legal representation is mandatory, the system will not replace a lawyer. It will prepare a timeline, case references, decisive documents, citations, contradictions, possible procedural routes and questions for professional verification.

## Economics and community sustainability

The business model can be expressed in one sentence: **the paid professional version will fund the free family version.**

### AI Advocate Family — free

One private family memory will be available to as many as 25 named members of an extended family. It will provide basic document intake, a family timeline, a relationship map and a shared private archive. It will not be funded by advertising, the sale of personal data or model training on private documents.

### AI Advocate Professional — paid

A sole trader, advisory centre, small employer, journalist, researcher or nonprofit organisation will purchase one professional account for the responsible operator; the base price will not multiply with the number of employees. The paid version will offer more working matters, higher processing volume, OCR and voice input, encrypted collaboration, audit records, professional exports, backup and technical support.

Larger institutions may later use separate licences and integrations. Grants, donations and partner licences can fund supported access for nonprofit advisory services and people without financial means.

Revenue should not remain solely with a central operator. Local advisory centres, social services, translators, professional guarantors and people with disabilities involved in testing can provide training and methodological support and receive agreed payment for that work. Users must be able to export their data so that they are not locked into a single provider.

A local-first architecture is not only a privacy measure. It also controls operating costs and helps keep basic family memory free without trading in personal data.

## Privacy and safety as core features

Basic processing should preferably take place on the user’s device. Sharing will be optional, encrypted and controlled through specific permissions. Users must be able to:

- decide which information, if any, leaves their device;
- choose who can see each part of the archive;
- revoke access at any time;
- export or delete their data;
- review access and change history;
- prohibit the use of private documents for model training.

The original document will remain private. Only a separate copy reviewed and anonymised by the system and expressly approved by a person may become public. My own archive may serve as a public reference case; information about other people remains private until a lawful basis and approved scope of disclosure have been established.

A user’s account remains a claim until supported by a document or another verifiable source. The system must not fill a missing event merely because it sounds plausible. Users must correct errors, provide documents that contradict their own position as well as those that support it, and must not knowingly give the system false information.

A responsible output should say:

**This is what you stated. This is what the documents establish. This has not yet been proved. This information is missing. A deadline may be running here. A professional must check this part before further action.**

## A citizen’s window into the work of legislators

Sometimes a person or nonprofit organisation does not need another legal analysis. They need to know which legislator is actually working on the issue and who may be worth approaching with documented evidence.

A future module will compare not only statements and campaign slogans but publicly traceable actions: votes, bills, amendments, committee work, parliamentary questions and differences between public statements and subsequent conduct. The output will not be political advertising or an instruction on how to vote, but a source-grounded overview including reasons, contradictions and uncertainty.

While developing the project, we used GPT-5.6 in this way to identify Members of the European Parliament who might be interested in the planned journey to Santiago and the development of an assistive device for people with disabilities. A cross-sectional view of their public work produced a clearer result than reading isolated statements one by one. The development goal is to extend this method across European countries wherever verifiable voting and legislative data are available.

## Conditional Geneva–Santiago field pilot

In spring 2027, I plan to walk approximately 2,000 kilometres from Geneva to Santiago de Compostela with my wife and our dogs. The journey is intended as field testing of accessible AI tools for people with disabilities when working with documents, communicating with institutions and planning accessible travel.

The system could continuously connect information about route surface and passability, obstacles, accessible toilets, water, accommodation, charging and service accessibility. One option is a specialised electric expedition wheelchair with a preliminary development and production estimate of approximately CZK 2 million; we do not currently have these funds.

The wheelchair is not a condition of developing AI Advocate or an automatic budget item. It would be used only if the project received support and the competition rules and organisers expressly permitted such expenditure. After the journey, it could remain in Spain for use by people with disabilities or pilgrims who need technical assistance.

## How we built the project

### Timeline and work contribution

Work began on 20 April 2026 with the practical processing and testing of a real archive, before we knew the rules of this competition. By 17 July 2026, this represented approximately 500 hours of collaborative work by the creator using OpenAI tools — GPT-5.6 and Codex. During this period, we developed the method, the connected case memory, document checks and source-grounded working outputs. We do not present this as work created during the competition period; it is the documented foundation on which the competition prototype could be safely built and tested.

From 17 to 20 July 2026, work focused specifically on OpenAI Build Week: product definition, improvements and safety boundaries for the public prototype, two controlled before-and-after tests, anonymisation of public evidence, the bilingual interface, documentation, deployment, 54 automated checks and production and verification of the demonstration video. We spent more than 50 hours preparing this competition version of the project. This figure describes the combined human work on the competition deliverables; it does not attempt to convert model processing into separate working hours.

GPT-5.6 and Codex were used within both periods. Their separate hourly contribution cannot be isolated precisely because model work occurred in response to human prompting, review, correction and decision-making. We therefore identify their specific roles instead and preserve a dated history of repository changes.

GPT-5.6 helped turn the broader social and legal problem into a product concept, distinguish facts, claims, procedural steps and legal conclusions, and design a source-grounded output structure.

Codex audited the repository and implemented the safety boundaries, case map, user interface, 54 automated checks, documentation and limited GitHub Pages deployment. During the final tests, it helped compare the archive before and after the arrival of a new record and prepare document-specific drafts for human review. The creator retained responsibility for final wording and submission.

No model runs inside the current static demonstration. This is an intentional safety boundary: prepared conclusions must not be transferred to unsupported documents. The next stage will connect a model only after input, citation and output-schema validation, with human review.

## What we learned and what comes next

Generating text is not the hardest problem. Maintaining trust is. Legal and personal documents are incomplete, use inconsistent names and refer to events from many years ago. A responsible system must preserve the source, show uncertainty and explain why it made a particular connection.

Institutional memory should not belong only to the State, a company or an organisation. An individual must also be able to discover what institutions knew, when they knew it and how their positions changed.

The next step is to turn the working prototype into a safe tool for individuals, families, advisory services and professionals. Its measure will be whether it helps answer four questions safely:

- What happened?
- What does the evidence show?
- What remains unknown?
- What must a person check?

AI Advocate must not manufacture truth. It must keep facts, evidence and the relationships between them connected — not inventions. Its goal is not to sound persuasive. Its goal is to be auditable.

## Current limitations

The public version accepts text for technical mapping and exactly one versioned court PDF, together with its anonymised derivative, for the prepared test. It does not contain general PDF/DOCX analysis, OCR, a database, authentication, universal legal analysis, voice input, an advisory network, legislator matching or automated filing. These are planned development tracks, not claims about the current prototype.
